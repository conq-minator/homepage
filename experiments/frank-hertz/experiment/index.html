Theoretical Foundation

According to the Bohr atomic model (proposed by Niels Bohr in 1913), electrons in an atom can orbit the nucleus in certain orbits (known as stationary orbits).

The model indicated the presence of discrete electronic energy levels in atoms, which could be computed on the hypothesis that the angular momentum of the orbiting electrons was quantized.

Moreover, electrons can only gain and lose energy by jumping from one allowed orbit to another, absorbing or emitting electromagnetic radiation with a frequency ν determined by the energy difference of the levels according to the relation:

ΔE = E₂ − E₁ = hν

It is then expected that transfer of energy to atomic electrons by any mechanism should always occur in discrete amounts. One such mechanism of energy transfer is through inelastic scattering of low-energy electrons.

Franck and Hertz first attempted to verify these ideas in 1914. The Franck-Hertz tube in the present experimental setup is a tetrode filled with Argon gas.

When electrons gain sufficient kinetic energy, they excite Argon atoms through inelastic collisions, resulting in characteristic decreases in collector current. By measuring collector current as a function of accelerating voltage, the excitation potential of Argon can be determined.

Result

From the experimentally obtained collector current versus accelerating voltage characteristic, a series of periodic current maxima and minima were observed, corresponding to successive inelastic collisions between accelerated electrons and argon atoms. The average spacing between adjacent peaks was found to be approximately 11.6 eV, which represents the first excitation potential of argon.

The experiment therefore verifies that atoms possess discrete energy levels and absorb energy only in quantized amounts. Hence, the Franck–Hertz experiment provides direct experimental confirmation of the quantized electronic energy levels predicted by quantum theory.

Key Finding

Quantized

The periodic nature of the current-voltage characteristic curve demonstrates that electron energy loss occurs in discrete steps, each corresponding to the excitation of argon atoms from their ground state to higher energy levels. This landmark experiment, first conducted by James Franck and Gustav Hertz in 1914, provided crucial experimental evidence supporting the quantum mechanical model of atomic structure and remains a cornerstone demonstration in atomic physics education.
