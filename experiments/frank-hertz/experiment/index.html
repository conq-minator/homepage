Frank-Hertz Experiment

Objective

To study the discrete energy levels of atoms by inelastic electron scattering in Argon gas and to determine the first excitation potential of Argon.

Theoretical Foundation

According to the Bohr atomic model, electrons in an atom can orbit the nucleus in certain stationary orbits. The model indicated the presence of discrete electronic energy levels in atoms.

Electrons can only gain and lose energy by jumping from one allowed orbit to another, absorbing or emitting electromagnetic radiation with a frequency ν determined by the energy difference of the levels according to the relation:

ΔE = E2 − E1 = hν

It is then expected that transfer of energy to atomic electrons by any mechanism should always occur in discrete amounts. One such mechanism of energy transfer is through inelastic scattering of low-energy electrons.

When electrons gain sufficient kinetic energy, they excite Argon atoms through inelastic collisions, resulting in characteristic decreases in collector current. By measuring collector current as a function of accelerating voltage, the excitation potential of Argon can be determined.

Working Formula

The spacing between successive peaks in the Franck-Hertz curve corresponds to the excitation energy of the atoms in the gas tube.

If ΔV is the average voltage difference between consecutive current peaks, the excitation potential is given by:

Excitation Potential (E) = e × ΔV

Where:

  • E = Excitation energy (in eV)
  • e = Elementary charge of an electron
  • ΔV = Average spacing between adjacent peaks (in Volts)

Calculations & Results

From the experimentally obtained collector current versus accelerating voltage characteristic, a series of periodic current maxima and minima were observed, corresponding to successive inelastic collisions between accelerated electrons and argon atoms. The average spacing between adjacent peaks was found to be approximately 11.6 eV, which represents the first excitation potential of argon.

First Excitation Potential
11.6 eV
Target Medium
Argon
Key Finding
Quantized Energy

The experiment therefore verifies that atoms possess discrete energy levels and absorb energy only in quantized amounts. Hence, the Franck–Hertz experiment provides direct experimental confirmation of the quantized electronic energy levels predicted by quantum theory.
