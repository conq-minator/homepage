Determination of Modulus of Rigidity by Static Method

Theory & Principle

The Modulus of Rigidity (or Shear Modulus), denoted by G or η, is defined as the ratio of shear stress to shear strain within the elastic limit of a material. It measures the rigidity of a body or its resistance to deformation caused by a shearing force.

In the static torsion method, a cylindrical rod is clamped solidly at one end and subjected to a twisting couple (torque) at the other end. This is achieved using a pulley and a suspended mass.

When a mass M is hung on a string wound around a pulley of radius R, the torque applied to the free end of the rod is:

Torque (τ) = M × g × R

Equating the applied torque to the restoring torque gives the formula for the Rigidity Modulus:

η = (180 × l × d × g) / (π2 × r4) × (m / φ)

Where:

  • m = Mass of the applied load in kg
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s²)
  • d = Diameter of the pulley in meters
  • l = Pin separation (length of experimental rod between pointers) in meters
  • r = Radius of the cross-section of the rod in meters
  • φ = The angle of twist measured in degrees

Apparatus Required

To successfully perform the static torsion experiment in a physical laboratory setting, the following equipment is required:

  • Static Torsion Apparatus: The core setup consisting of a heavy cast-iron or steel frame. It features a mechanism to solidly clamp the experimental cylindrical rod at one end, while the other end is attached to a grooved pulley wheel.
  • Slotted Weights and Hanger: A set of standard cast-iron or brass masses (commonly in 500g increments) and a hanger. These are used to apply a precise downward gravitational force, which creates the twisting torque on the pulley.
  • Pointers and Circular Scales: Two metallic pointers clamped to the experimental rod at specific distances. They move across fixed, graduated circular scales (often marked in degrees or half-degrees) to measure the angle of twist at different points along the rod.
  • Vernier Caliper: A high-precision measurement tool used to accurately measure the diameter of the experimental rod and the diameter of the pulley. These measurements are crucial for calculating the radii (r and R).
  • Meter Scale or Measuring Tape: A standard metric ruler used to measure the working pin separation (L), which is the exact distance between the two pointers.
  • Inextensible String: A strong, thin cord wrapped around the groove of the pulley to connect it to the weight hanger. It must not stretch under the applied load.

Experimental Procedure

Follow these steps to conduct the virtual experiment:

  1. Apparatus Setup: Navigate to the Experiment Simulation tab. You will see a rod fixed at the right end and attached to a pulley wheel at the left end.
  2. Set Parameters: In the Data Input & Control panel, select the material of the rod and specify the Pin Separation L.
  3. Measurements: Open the Measurements tab. Use the Screw Gauge and Vernier Caliper to measure rod diameter and pulley diameter (four readings each). Averaged values unlock the experiment and populate the simulation automatically.
  4. Initial Reading: Ensure the live load (M) is set to 0.0 kg. Click Save Data Point to log your zero-error state.
  5. Loading the Rod: Click the + button on the slider to add weights in 0.5 kg increments.
  6. Record Observations: As you add weight, the rod will twist. Observe the readings on Scale 1 (θ1) and Scale 2 (θ2) measured in degrees. The zoomed-in dials will clearly show this angular deflection.
  7. Save Data: Click Save Data Point. This will log the current load, angles, and calculated Modulus of Rigidity (G) into the observation table.
  8. Plot Graph: Continue adding weights up to 5.0 kg (or until the scale limit is reached), saving data at regular intervals. A graph of Mean Deflection vs. Load will plot automatically. It should represent a straight line passing through the origin, verifying Hooke's Law for torsion.
